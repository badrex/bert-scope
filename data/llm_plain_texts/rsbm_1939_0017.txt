He led a double life as a respected figure in both malacology and pathology, with a wide knowledge of biology that characterized his writings and teaching. Boycott held various positions in prestigious institutions, including the University of Manchester and University College Medical School, where he conducted research and taught pathology. He also served on the councils of the Royal Society and the Medical Research Council, among other organizations.'
